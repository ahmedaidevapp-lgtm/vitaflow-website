Vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D): Normal Range, What High & Low Levels Mean

Your body depends on vitamin D for far more than strong bones — this powerhouse nutrient helps you absorb calcium, keeps your immune system sharp, and supports muscle function every single day. Yet vitamin D deficiency is remarkably common, often developing silently before any symptoms appear. The Vitamin D test, formally called the 25-hydroxyvitamin D test, gives your doctor a reliable snapshot of your total vitamin D status by measuring the main form circulating in your blood. Whether you spend most of your day indoors, live in a region with limited sunshine, or simply want to understand a recent lab result, this page walks you through exactly what your number means.

What Is Vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D)?

The 25-hydroxyvitamin D test measures the main circulating form of vitamin D in the blood, reflecting total vitamin D status from both sun exposure and dietary intake; vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption, bone health, and immune function.

Vitamin D is a fat-soluble nutrient your body gets from two main sources: sunlight hitting your skin and the food or supplements you consume. When either source delivers vitamin D, your liver converts it into a molecule called 25-hydroxyvitamin D — sometimes written as 25-OH vitamin D or calcidiol. This is the form the test measures, and it reflects your overall vitamin D stores more accurately than any other blood marker. Doctors order this test for several reasons: to screen people at higher risk of deficiency, to check in on bone health, to investigate unexplained fatigue or muscle weakness, or to monitor a patient who is already taking vitamin D supplements. Understanding your result comes down to a few key thresholds, all measured in nanograms per milliliter (ng/mL). According to the Endocrine Society, a level below 20 ng/mL is considered deficient — meaning your body does not have enough vitamin D to meet its basic needs. A reading between 20 and 29 ng/mL falls into the insufficient range, where stores are low but not critically so. Reaching 30 ng/mL or above puts you in the sufficient range, which spans up to 100 ng/mL and represents the zone where vitamin D is doing its job properly. Levels above 100 ng/mL move into potentially toxic territory, a situation almost always tied to taking very high doses of supplements over time rather than anything your skin or diet alone could produce. One number never tells the whole story on its own. Your result makes the most sense when a clinician considers it alongside your lifestyle, health history, symptoms, and any medications you take.

What Causes Abnormal Vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D) Levels?

Common Causes of High Vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D)

  • Excessive vitamin D supplementation (most common cause of toxicity)
  • Granulomatous diseases (sarcoidosis, tuberculosis, histoplasmosis — these convert vitamin D to its active form without normal regulation)
  • Certain lymphomas
  • Primary hyperparathyroidism (rare)

Source: Endocrine Society. Evaluation, Treatment, and Prevention of Vitamin D Deficiency. 2011. https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/96/7/1911/2833671

Common Causes of Low Vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D)

  • Insufficient sun exposure (key source of vitamin D)
  • Darker skin pigmentation (melanin reduces skin synthesis)
  • Older age (skin synthesis declines with age)
  • Obesity (vitamin D is fat-soluble and sequestered in adipose tissue)
  • Malabsorption disorders (celiac disease, Crohn's disease, cystic fibrosis)
  • Chronic kidney disease (impaired conversion to active vitamin D)
  • Liver disease
  • Vegan diet without supplementation
  • Certain medications (corticosteroids, antiepileptics, cholestyramine)

Source: Endocrine Society. Evaluation, Treatment, and Prevention of Vitamin D Deficiency. 2011. https://academic.oup.com/jcem/article/96/7/1911/2833671

Symptoms Associated with Vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D) Imbalance

Symptoms of High Vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D)

  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Weakness and fatigue
  • Frequent urination and excessive thirst
  • Kidney stones (from hypercalcaemia caused by excess vitamin D)
  • Confusion and disorientation in severe cases

Symptoms of Low Vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D)

  • Bone pain and tenderness (particularly in adults: osteomalacia)
  • Muscle weakness and cramps
  • Fatigue
  • Low mood and depression
  • Frequent infections (vitamin D supports immune function)
  • In severe deficiency in children: rickets (bowed legs, soft skull bones)

Source: NHS. Vitamin D deficiency. https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/vitamins-and-minerals/vitamin-d/

Frequently Asked Questions About Vitamin D (25-hydroxyvitamin D)

What does a vitamin D blood test actually measure?

The test measures 25-hydroxyvitamin D — also called 25-OH vitamin D or calcidiol — the main form of vitamin D circulating in your blood. Your liver produces this molecule from the vitamin D your skin makes during sun exposure and from what you take in through food or supplements. Because it captures both of those sources together, it gives the clearest picture of your overall vitamin D status. Doctors use this single marker to understand whether your body has enough vitamin D to support calcium absorption, bone strength, and immune function. Discuss your specific result with your healthcare provider to understand what it means for you personally.

What is the normal range for vitamin D?

According to the Endocrine Society, a sufficient vitamin D level for adults sits between 30 and 100 ng/mL. Levels from 20 to 29 ng/mL are classified as insufficient — lower than ideal but not severely so. A reading below 20 ng/mL is defined as deficient. At the other end, levels above 100 ng/mL are considered potentially toxic. Keep in mind that individual laboratories may use slightly different reference intervals, and what counts as optimal for you can depend on factors like your age, health conditions, and any symptoms you have. Your healthcare provider is the right person to interpret exactly where your number falls for your situation.

What causes vitamin D levels to be too high?

The most common reason vitamin D climbs above 100 ng/mL — the threshold the Endocrine Society flags as potentially toxic — is taking excessive amounts of vitamin D supplements over time. Certain medical conditions can also push levels up, including granulomatous diseases such as sarcoidosis, tuberculosis, and histoplasmosis, which convert vitamin D to its active form without the body's usual regulatory checks. Some types of lymphoma and, rarely, primary hyperparathyroidism can have the same effect. Because toxicity from sun exposure alone is not considered a realistic concern, a very high result almost always prompts questions about supplement use or an underlying condition. Talk to your healthcare provider about what may be driving your elevated level.

What causes low vitamin D levels?

Many factors can pull vitamin D levels below the sufficient range. Limited sun exposure is a key driver — whether from working indoors, living at high latitudes, or covering your skin. Darker skin pigmentation naturally reduces how much vitamin D your skin synthesizes, and this capacity also declines with age. Because vitamin D is fat-soluble, it can become sequestered in body fat, making obesity a contributing factor. Conditions that impair absorption — such as celiac disease, Crohn's disease, and cystic fibrosis — prevent the gut from taking in enough dietary vitamin D. Chronic kidney disease and liver disease interfere with how the body processes vitamin D. A vegan diet without supplementation and certain medications, including corticosteroids and some antiepileptics, can also lower levels. Speak with your healthcare provider to identify what might be affecting your result.

What symptoms are linked to low or high vitamin D?

Low vitamin D can show up as bone pain and tenderness (a condition in adults called osteomalacia), muscle weakness and cramps, persistent fatigue, low mood, and more frequent infections, since vitamin D plays a role in immune function. In children, severe deficiency can cause rickets, which affects bone development. On the opposite end, very high vitamin D levels may cause nausea, vomiting, weakness, fatigue, frequent urination, excessive thirst, and kidney stones — effects driven by the excess calcium that builds up when vitamin D is too high. Confusion and disorientation can occur in severe cases. Many of these symptoms overlap with other conditions, so a blood test is the only reliable way to know your actual vitamin D status. Always discuss any symptoms with your healthcare provider.

Should I retest my vitamin D after making lifestyle or supplement changes?

Retesting is a common next step after a low or high result, especially if you have changed your sun exposure habits, adjusted your diet, or started or stopped a supplement. The timing of a follow-up test matters — vitamin D levels shift gradually over weeks to months rather than days, so testing too soon may not capture meaningful change. The right retesting interval depends on your starting level, what changes you made, and any underlying health conditions that affect how your body processes vitamin D. There is no single answer that fits everyone, which is why a personalised plan from your healthcare provider is the most reliable guide for deciding when to recheck your level.
