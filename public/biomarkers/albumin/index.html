Albumin: Normal Range, What High & Low Levels Mean

If you've ever received a blood test panel, you may have spotted a line labeled albumin — but what exactly does it mean? Albumin is the most abundant protein circulating in your bloodstream, and it quietly does an enormous amount of work every day. Produced entirely by your liver, it helps keep fluid where it belongs, ferries hormones and medications through your body, and gives doctors a reliable window into your nutritional health and how well your liver is functioning. Understanding your albumin result can help you have a more informed conversation with your doctor.

What Is Albumin?

Albumin is the most abundant protein in the blood, produced exclusively by the liver; it maintains fluid balance between blood vessels and tissues, transports hormones, drugs, and fatty acids, and its level reflects both nutritional status and liver synthetic function.

Think of albumin as your blood's hardest-working protein. Your liver manufactures it continuously, and once it enters your bloodstream it takes on several jobs at once: it acts like a sponge, holding fluid inside your blood vessels so it doesn't leak into surrounding tissues; it acts like a shuttle bus, carrying hormones, drugs, and fatty acids to the cells that need them; and because the liver makes it, its level tells your doctor how well your liver is doing its job and whether your body is getting enough protein from food. Doctors order an albumin test for many reasons — as part of a routine metabolic panel, to check liver or kidney health, to assess nutritional status before surgery, or to help explain unexplained swelling or fatigue. The test itself is a simple blood draw. For adults aged 18 and older, the normal reference range is 3.4 to 5.4 g/dL (grams per deciliter). A result within that window generally means your liver is producing albumin at a healthy rate and your protein nutrition is adequate. A result below 3.4 g/dL is considered low — doctors call this hypoalbuminemia — and can signal liver disease, poor nutrition, kidney problems that allow albumin to spill into urine, or a significant inflammatory illness. A result above 5.4 g/dL is considered high and most commonly points to dehydration, where the fluid around the protein has decreased, making the concentration appear elevated rather than truly increased. One number never tells the whole story. Your doctor will read your albumin result alongside your symptoms, your other lab values, and your medical history before drawing any conclusions. What Causes Abnormal Albumin Levels?

Common Causes of High Albumin

  • Dehydration (most common cause — concentrated blood elevates albumin)
  • High-protein diet combined with dehydration
  • Prolonged use of a tourniquet when drawing blood (spurious elevation)

Source: MedlinePlus. Albumin Blood Test. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/albumin-blood-test/

Common Causes of Low Albumin

  • Liver disease (cirrhosis, hepatitis — reduced albumin synthesis)
  • Malnutrition or prolonged fasting
  • Nephrotic syndrome (albumin lost in urine)
  • Inflammatory states (albumin is a negative acute-phase reactant — it falls with severe illness)
  • Inflammatory bowel disease and protein-losing enteropathy
  • Burns (major burns cause massive albumin loss)
  • Heart failure
  • Hypothyroidism

Source: NHS. Liver blood tests. https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/liver-disease/diagnosis/

Symptoms Associated with Albumin Imbalance

Symptoms of High Albumin

  • Symptoms of dehydration: thirst, dark urine, dry mouth
  • High albumin alone rarely causes symptoms

Symptoms of Low Albumin

  • Oedema — swelling of the ankles, feet, or abdomen (ascites)
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Muscle wasting and unintentional weight loss
  • Poor wound healing
  • Increased susceptibility to infections
  • Brittle nails and thinning hair

Source: NHS. Liver disease — symptoms. https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/liver-disease/symptoms/

Frequently Asked Questions About Albumin

What does albumin actually measure?

Albumin measures the level of the most abundant protein in your blood, which your liver produces. It has three main roles: keeping fluid balanced between your blood vessels and surrounding tissues, transporting hormones, drugs, and fatty acids around your body, and reflecting how well your liver is synthesizing protein. Because the liver is solely responsible for making albumin, the result gives doctors a useful snapshot of both your liver's function and your overall nutritional status. Low or high readings can each point to specific underlying processes worth investigating. Always discuss your individual result with your healthcare provider.

What is the normal albumin range for adults?

For adults aged 18 and older, the standard reference range for albumin in the blood is 3.4 to 5.4 g/dL (grams per deciliter), according to Mayo Clinic Laboratories. A result that falls anywhere within that window is generally considered normal. Keep in mind that reference ranges can vary slightly between laboratories depending on the equipment and methods they use, so always interpret your result against the range printed on your own lab report. A value inside the normal range is reassuring but not the whole picture — your healthcare provider is best placed to interpret it alongside your other results.

What does high albumin mean?

A result above 5.4 g/dL is considered elevated. The most common reason is dehydration — when the fluid volume in your blood decreases, the concentration of albumin rises even though your liver hasn't produced more of it. A high-protein diet combined with dehydration can have the same effect. A technical factor can also play a role: if a tourniquet was left on your arm too long during the blood draw, it can cause a spuriously high reading that doesn't reflect your true level. High albumin on its own rarely causes symptoms, and the finding often resolves once hydration is restored. Discuss your result with your healthcare provider to identify the most likely cause.

What causes low albumin levels?

Low albumin — a result below 3.4 g/dL — can develop for several reasons. Liver diseases such as cirrhosis or hepatitis reduce the liver's ability to synthesize albumin. Malnutrition or prolonged fasting means the body lacks the raw protein materials to make it. Nephrotic syndrome causes albumin to leak out through the kidneys into urine. Severe or prolonged illness triggers an inflammatory response that actively lowers albumin levels. Conditions like inflammatory bowel disease, protein-losing enteropathy, major burns, heart failure, and hypothyroidism can all contribute as well. Because so many different conditions can lower albumin, your healthcare provider will use additional tests and your clinical history to identify the underlying cause.

What symptoms are associated with abnormal albumin levels?

When albumin is low, the most visible sign is often oedema — noticeable swelling in your ankles, feet, or abdomen (called ascites) — because albumin is no longer holding fluid inside your blood vessels. You might also notice fatigue, muscle wasting, unintentional weight loss, wounds that are slow to heal, a greater susceptibility to infections, brittle nails, or thinning hair. When albumin is high due to dehydration, you may experience thirst, dark-coloured urine, or a dry mouth. These symptoms overlap with many other conditions, so a single symptom is never a diagnosis on its own. Please speak with your healthcare provider about any symptoms you're experiencing alongside your albumin result.

Should I be worried if my albumin is slightly outside the normal range?

A single result that sits just outside the reference range doesn't automatically mean something is seriously wrong. Lab values can shift due to hydration status, the timing of your last meal, a recent illness, or even how the sample was collected. What matters more is the pattern over time and how the number fits with your overall health picture — your symptoms, your other lab values, and your medical history.
