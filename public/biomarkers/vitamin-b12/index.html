Vitamin B12: Normal Range, What High & Low Levels Mean

Your blood carries a remarkable nutrient called Vitamin B12 — also known as cobalamin — that quietly keeps some of your body's most critical systems running. It helps your body make healthy red blood cells, build and repair DNA, and keep your nerves working properly. A simple blood test can reveal how much B12 is circulating in your system right now. Whether your doctor ordered this test as part of a routine check-up or because you've been feeling off lately, understanding what your result means is the first step toward taking charge of your health.

What Is Vitamin B12?

Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) is a water-soluble vitamin essential for the formation of red blood cells, DNA synthesis, and the maintenance of the nervous system; this test measures the amount circulating in the blood.

Vitamin B12, sometimes called cobalamin or serum B12, is a water-soluble vitamin your body cannot make on its own — you have to get it from food or supplements. It plays three essential roles: helping form red blood cells, supporting the synthesis of DNA (the genetic instructions inside every cell), and maintaining the health of your nervous system. Because it does so much, even a modest shortfall can affect how you feel from head to toe. Doctors order a B12 test for many reasons. You might be experiencing unexplained fatigue, tingling in your hands or feet, or mood changes that don't have an obvious cause. Your doctor might also check it if you follow a vegan or strict vegetarian diet, take certain long-term medications like metformin or proton pump inhibitors, are older, or have a condition that affects nutrient absorption, such as Crohn's disease or celiac disease. The test measures the amount of B12 circulating in your blood at the time of the draw. For adults aged 18 and over, the normal reference range used by Mayo Clinic Laboratories runs from 200 to 900 picograms per milliliter — often written as pg/mL. A picogram is an extremely tiny unit of weight, which gives you a sense of just how little B12 the body actually needs, and yet how consequential even small changes in that level can be. A result that falls within 200–900 pg/mL generally suggests your body has adequate B12 circulating. A result below 200 pg/mL may indicate deficiency, while a result above 900 pg/mL is considered elevated. Both ends of the spectrum can carry meaningful information, which is why your doctor looks at this number alongside your symptoms, diet, medications, and other test results rather than in isolation.

What Causes Abnormal Vitamin B12 Levels?

Common Causes of High Vitamin B12

  • Vitamin B12 supplementation or injection
  • Liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis — the liver stores B12 and releases it when damaged)
  • Myeloproliferative disorders (CML, polycythaemia vera)
  • Solid tumours (some cancers release B12-binding proteins)
  • Kidney disease

Source: MedlinePlus. Vitamin B12 Test. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/vitamin-b12-test/

Common Causes of Low Vitamin B12

  • Vegan or strict vegetarian diet (B12 is found almost exclusively in animal products)
  • Pernicious anaemia (autoimmune condition destroying intrinsic factor needed for B12 absorption — most common cause in older adults)
  • Gastric surgery or atrophic gastritis (reduced intrinsic factor production)
  • Malabsorption disorders (celiac disease, Crohn's disease)
  • Metformin use (long-term use impairs B12 absorption)
  • Older age (reduced stomach acid impairs absorption)
  • Proton pump inhibitor (PPI) long-term use

Source: NHS. Vitamin B12 or folate deficiency anaemia. https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/vitamin-b12-or-folate-deficiency-anaemia/

Symptoms Associated with Vitamin B12 Imbalance

Symptoms of High Vitamin B12

  • High B12 from supplementation typically causes no symptoms
  • Very high levels from liver or blood disorders may be associated with symptoms of those conditions

Symptoms of Low Vitamin B12

  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Numbness or tingling in hands and feet (peripheral neuropathy)
  • Difficulty walking and balance problems
  • Sore and inflamed tongue (glossitis)
  • Mouth ulcers
  • Pale or slightly yellow skin
  • Irritability and changes in mood
  • Memory problems and cognitive decline
  • Blurred vision
  • Megaloblastic anaemia: shortness of breath, headache, rapid heartbeat

Source: NHS. Vitamin B12 or folate deficiency anaemia — symptoms. https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/vitamin-b12-or-folate-deficiency-anaemia/symptoms/

Frequently Asked Questions About Vitamin B12

What does a Vitamin B12 blood test actually measure?

A Vitamin B12 blood test measures the amount of B12 — also called cobalamin — circulating in your bloodstream at the moment the sample is taken. B12 is a water-soluble vitamin your body relies on for three core jobs: making healthy red blood cells, synthesizing DNA, and keeping your nervous system working properly. Because your body can't manufacture B12 itself, the level in your blood reflects how much you've been absorbing from food or supplements over time. The test gives your doctor a snapshot of your current B12 status. Talk with your healthcare provider about what your specific result means for your situation.

What is the normal range for Vitamin B12 in a blood test?

For adults aged 18 and over, Mayo Clinic Laboratories defines the normal reference range for Vitamin B12 as 200 to 900 picograms per milliliter (pg/mL). A picogram is a tiny unit of measurement, which underscores just how little B12 the body needs — yet even small deviations matter. A result sitting comfortably between those two numbers generally suggests your circulating B12 is adequate. Keep in mind that reference ranges can vary slightly between laboratories depending on the method they use to run the test. Your lab report will show the range specific to where your sample was processed. Your healthcare provider is best placed to interpret your exact number in the context of your health history.

What does it mean if my Vitamin B12 level is high?

A B12 result above 900 pg/mL is considered elevated. The most straightforward explanation is that you've been taking B12 supplements or receiving B12 injections — in that case, a high reading is often expected and typically causes no symptoms on its own. However, unexpectedly high B12 levels not explained by supplementation can sometimes be linked to liver conditions such as hepatitis or cirrhosis, certain blood disorders like chronic myelogenous leukemia or polycythaemia vera, some solid tumours, or kidney disease. Because some of these causes are serious, an unexpectedly high result warrants a conversation with your healthcare provider to explore what might be driving the elevation.

What causes a low Vitamin B12 level?

Several things can push your B12 below 200 pg/mL. Since B12 is found almost exclusively in animal products, following a vegan or strict vegetarian diet is a common cause. The most frequent cause in older adults is pernicious anaemia — an autoimmune condition where the body destroys intrinsic factor, a protein you need to absorb B12 in your gut. Gastric surgery, atrophic gastritis, and malabsorption conditions like celiac disease or Crohn's disease can have a similar effect. Long-term use of metformin (a diabetes medication) or proton pump inhibitors (PPIs, used for acid reflux) can also impair absorption over time. Reduced stomach acid with older age is another factor. Your healthcare provider can help identify the underlying cause in your case.

What symptoms are linked to low Vitamin B12?

Low B12 can produce a wide range of symptoms because the vitamin affects both your blood and your nervous system. You might notice persistent fatigue and weakness, numbness or tingling in your hands and feet, difficulty walking or keeping your balance, a sore and inflamed tongue, or mouth ulcers. Some people develop pale or slightly yellow skin, irritability, mood changes, memory problems, or blurred vision. In more significant deficiency, a condition called megaloblastic anaemia can develop, which may cause shortness of breath, headaches, and a rapid heartbeat. Symptoms often develop gradually and can be easy to overlook. If any of these sound familiar, bring them up with your healthcare provider alongside your B12 result.

Should I retest my Vitamin B12 level, and how often?

How often you should retest depends entirely on your individual circumstances — your diet, any underlying conditions, medications you take, your age, and the reason your doctor ordered the test in the first place. Someone who has just started B12 supplementation, for example, may retest in a few months to confirm levels are rising. Someone managing a chronic condition affecting absorption might need more regular monitoring. There's no universal retesting schedule that fits everyone. Rather than going by a general rule, the best approach is to ask your healthcare provider when a follow-up test makes sense for you and what result they'd want to see at that point.
