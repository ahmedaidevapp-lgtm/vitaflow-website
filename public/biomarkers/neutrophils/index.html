Neutrophils: Normal Range, What High & Low Levels Mean

When your doctor orders a complete blood count, one of the numbers you might see is your neutrophil count — and it tells a surprisingly detailed story about how your immune system is doing. Neutrophils are the most abundant white blood cells in your body, and they act as your immune system's first responders, rushing to the scene whenever bacteria invade or tissue gets injured. Whether your result comes back as a raw number or a percentage, understanding what neutrophils do and what shifts in their count can signal helps you have a more informed conversation with your doctor.

What Is Neutrophils?

Neutrophils are the most abundant type of white blood cell and the first responders to bacterial infection and tissue injury; the neutrophil count (reported as an absolute number or percentage of total WBC) reflects the state of the innate immune system.

Neutrophils are a type of white blood cell — the kind your body produces in the largest quantities. Think of them as your immune system's rapid-response team. The moment bacteria enter your body or tissue gets damaged, neutrophils are among the very first cells to arrive and start fighting. Because they belong to what scientists call the innate immune system — your body's built-in, always-on defense network — their count gives doctors a quick snapshot of how that defense system is functioning right now. Your lab report may show two neutrophil values. The absolute neutrophil count, often abbreviated ANC, tells you the actual number of neutrophils in a small volume of blood, measured in thousands of cells per microliter (×10³/µL). The percentage value tells you what share of all your white blood cells are neutrophils. For adults, a healthy absolute count generally falls between 1.8 and 7.7 ×10³/µL, and a healthy percentage sits between 40% and 70% of total white blood cells, according to Mayo Clinic Laboratories. Doctors order neutrophil counts as part of routine checkups, but also when they suspect an infection, are monitoring you through chemotherapy, or are investigating unexplained fatigue, recurrent infections, or fever. A result outside the typical range is not a diagnosis on its own — it is a clue that prompts your doctor to look deeper. Many everyday factors, from strenuous exercise to certain medications to being pregnant, can nudge your count in one direction or the other. Your result only becomes meaningful when your doctor weighs it alongside your symptoms, your medical history, and any other test results.

What Causes Abnormal Neutrophils Levels?

Common Causes of High Neutrophils

  • Bacterial infection (neutrophilia is the hallmark response)
  • Physical stress (surgery, trauma, burns, heart attack)
  • Corticosteroid medications (demarginate stored neutrophils)
  • Inflammatory conditions (gout, rheumatoid arthritis)
  • Smoking
  • Strenuous exercise
  • Chronic myeloid leukaemia (very high counts)
  • Pregnancy

Source: MedlinePlus. Differential Blood Count. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/differential-blood-count/

Common Causes of Low Neutrophils

  • Viral infections (influenza, HIV, hepatitis)
  • Chemotherapy and radiation
  • Certain medications (clozapine, carbimazole, some antibiotics)
  • Bone marrow failure (aplastic anaemia)
  • Autoimmune neutropenia (antibodies destroy neutrophils)
  • Severe bacterial infection (can deplete neutrophils)
  • Vitamin B12 or folate deficiency
  • Felty's syndrome (rheumatoid arthritis + enlarged spleen)

Source: NHLBI. Aplastic Anemia. https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/health/aplastic-anemia

Symptoms Associated with Neutrophils Imbalance

Symptoms of High Neutrophils

  • Symptoms of the underlying cause: fever, pain, and swelling in infection
  • High neutrophils alone do not cause specific symptoms

Symptoms of Low Neutrophils

  • Frequent or severe bacterial or fungal infections
  • Mouth ulcers (aphthous ulcers)
  • Skin infections and slow-healing wounds
  • Fever without obvious source
  • Fatigue

Source: NHLBI. Aplastic Anemia — Symptoms. https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/health/aplastic-anemia/symptoms

Frequently Asked Questions About Neutrophils

What are neutrophils and what does a neutrophil blood test measure?

Neutrophils are the most abundant type of white blood cell in your blood, and their primary job is to act as first responders whenever bacteria invade or tissue gets injured. A neutrophil test — usually run as part of a complete blood count with differential — measures either the absolute number of neutrophils in your blood (called the absolute neutrophil count, or ANC), the percentage of your white blood cells that are neutrophils, or both. Together, these numbers reflect the current state of your innate immune system, which is your body's built-in, always-active defense network. Talk with your healthcare provider about what your specific result means for you.

What is the normal range for neutrophils in adults?

According to Mayo Clinic Laboratories, the standard reference range for adults eighteen years and older is 1.8 to 7.7 ×10³/µL for the absolute neutrophil count — that means between 1,800 and 7,700 neutrophil cells per microliter of blood. When expressed as a percentage of all white blood cells, the typical range is 40% to 70%. Keep in mind that reference ranges can vary slightly between laboratories depending on the equipment and methods they use, so the range printed on your own lab report is the most relevant benchmark for your result. Your healthcare provider is best placed to tell you whether your specific number falls where it should for your age and health status.

What causes high neutrophils?

A neutrophil count above the upper end of the typical range is called neutrophilia. The most common trigger is a bacterial infection, because your immune system rapidly produces more neutrophils to fight the invading bacteria. Physical stress on the body — such as surgery, trauma, burns, or a heart attack — can also drive the count up, as can inflammatory conditions like gout or rheumatoid arthritis. Corticosteroid medications, smoking, strenuous exercise, and pregnancy are other recognized causes. In rare cases, a very high count may point to a bone marrow condition such as chronic myeloid leukaemia. Because many different situations can raise neutrophils, a high result needs context from your full clinical picture. Discuss what may be driving your result with your healthcare provider.

What causes low neutrophils (neutropenia)?

When your neutrophil count drops below the lower end of the typical range, the condition is called neutropenia. Viral infections — including influenza, HIV, and hepatitis — are a common cause, because viruses can temporarily suppress white blood cell production. Chemotherapy and radiation are well-known causes because they affect fast-dividing cells in the bone marrow. Certain medications, including clozapine, carbimazole, and some antibiotics, can also lower the count. Less commonly, neutropenia may result from bone marrow failure, autoimmune conditions where the body attacks its own neutrophils, vitamin B12 or folate deficiency, or a rare condition called Felty's syndrome. A single low reading doesn't automatically signal a serious problem, but it warrants follow-up. Please speak with your healthcare provider to understand the likely cause in your case.

What symptoms are linked to abnormal neutrophil levels?

High neutrophils on their own do not typically cause specific symptoms — what you feel is usually related to the underlying cause, such as fever, pain, and swelling during a bacterial infection. Low neutrophils, on the other hand, can leave your body less equipped to fend off pathogens, and this can show up in recognizable ways. People with low neutrophil counts may experience frequent or unusually severe bacterial or fungal infections, mouth ulcers, skin infections, wounds that heal slowly, fever without an obvious source, and general fatigue. These symptoms overlap with many other conditions, so experiencing them does not confirm neutropenia. If any of these symptoms sound familiar, contact your healthcare provider so they can evaluate your full blood picture.

Should I be worried if my neutrophil count is slightly outside the normal range?

A result that sits just outside the reference range is not automatically a cause for alarm. Lab reference ranges are built from large populations of healthy adults, which means a small percentage of perfectly healthy people will naturally fall slightly above or below the boundary. Temporary factors like a recent workout, mild illness, stress, or certain medications can shift your count without indicating a lasting problem. What matters most is the trend over time, how far outside the range your value sits, and whether the result aligns with any symptoms or other findings. A one-time borderline result often warrants a repeat test rather than immediate concern. Your healthcare provider is the right person to put your specific number in context and decide whether any follow-up is needed.
