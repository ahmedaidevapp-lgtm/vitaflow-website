Serum Iron: Normal Range, What High & Low Levels Mean

Iron is one of the most essential minerals your body relies on — it helps carry oxygen through your blood, powers your muscles, and keeps your energy levels steady. A serum iron test (also called a blood iron test, iron blood test, or Fe test) gives your doctor a snapshot of how much iron is actively circulating in your bloodstream right now. Whether you've been feeling unusually tired, your doctor suspects you may be getting too much iron, or you're simply monitoring a known condition, this single number can tell a meaningful story about your health. Read on to learn what serum iron actually measures and what your result might mean.

What Is Serum Iron?

Serum iron measures the amount of iron currently circulating in the blood bound to transferrin; it reflects the immediate iron supply available to cells but does not indicate total body iron stores the way ferritin does.

Think of your blood as a delivery network. Iron travels through it attached to a protein called transferrin — like a package strapped to a courier. Serum iron measures exactly how much iron is riding on those couriers at the moment your blood was drawn. It reflects the immediate iron supply available to your cells, so it captures what is circulating right now rather than how much iron your body has stored over time. That longer-term storage picture comes from a different test called ferritin, which doctors often order alongside serum iron to get the full story. Doctors order this test for many reasons: to investigate unexplained fatigue, to work up suspected anemia (a shortage of healthy red blood cells), to check for iron overload, or to monitor someone already being treated for an iron-related condition. Because serum iron can swing up and down with meals, hydration, and even the time of day, your doctor usually looks at it together with other iron-panel tests rather than in isolation. For adult males aged 18 and older, the normal range is 65 to 176 micrograms per deciliter (mcg/dL). For adult females aged 18 and older, the normal range is 50 to 170 mcg/dL. A result below those lower limits suggests your circulating iron supply may be running low, while a result above the upper limits points to more iron in the blood than your body typically needs. Either direction has its own set of possible causes, and neither a high nor a low number is a diagnosis on its own — it is a signal that prompts your doctor to ask more questions. Always review your specific number with your healthcare provider, who can interpret it alongside your full lab panel and personal health history.

What Causes Abnormal Serum Iron Levels?

Common Causes of High Serum Iron

  • Hereditary hemochromatosis (genetic iron overload)
  • Iron supplementation or overdose
  • Repeated blood transfusions
  • Liver disease (hepatitis, cirrhosis — releases stored iron)
  • Hemolytic anemia (red cell destruction releases iron)
  • Sideroblastic anemia
  • Thalassemia

Source: NHS. Haemochromatosis. https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/haemochromatosis/

Common Causes of Low Serum Iron

  • Iron deficiency (dietary or from blood loss)
  • Iron deficiency anemia
  • Chronic inflammatory diseases (iron sequestered as acute-phase response)
  • Malabsorption disorders (celiac disease, Crohn's disease)
  • Pregnancy (increased iron demand)
  • Chronic kidney disease
  • Hypothyroidism

Source: WHO. Iron Deficiency Anaemia: Assessment, Prevention, and Control. 2001. https://www.who.int/publications/i/item/9241547014

Symptoms Associated with Serum Iron Imbalance

Symptoms of High Serum Iron

  • Often asymptomatic at mildly elevated levels
  • Joint pain
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Abdominal pain
  • Skin bronzing or darkening (in chronic overload)
  • Liver enlargement

Symptoms of Low Serum Iron

  • Fatigue and tiredness
  • Pale skin
  • Shortness of breath
  • Brittle nails and hair loss
  • Cold intolerance
  • Brain fog and poor concentration
  • Restless leg syndrome

Source: NHLBI. Iron-Deficiency Anemia. https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/health/anemia/iron-deficiency-anemia

Frequently Asked Questions About Serum Iron

What does a serum iron test actually measure?

A serum iron test measures the amount of iron currently circulating in your blood — specifically, iron that is bound to a transport protein called transferrin. Think of transferrin as a shuttle bus: serum iron counts how many passengers (iron molecules) are on the bus right now. This is different from ferritin, which reflects your long-term iron reserves. Because serum iron only captures a moment-in-time snapshot, doctors typically pair it with other tests to get the complete picture of your iron status. Talk to your healthcare provider about what your serum iron result means alongside your other lab values.

What is a normal serum iron level?

Normal serum iron ranges differ slightly by sex. For adult males aged 18 and older, the reference range is 65 to 176 mcg/dL (micrograms per deciliter). For adult females aged 18 and older, it is 50 to 170 mcg/dL. These ranges come from Mayo Clinic Laboratories. A result within these boundaries suggests your circulating iron supply is in a healthy zone, though lab ranges can vary slightly between testing facilities. A result that falls just outside the range does not automatically mean something is wrong. Your healthcare provider is the right person to put your specific number in context.

What does it mean if my serum iron is high?

A serum iron result above the upper limit of the normal range means more iron than usual is circulating in your blood at the time of the draw. Several things can drive this, including a genetic condition called hereditary hemochromatosis (where the body absorbs too much iron), iron supplement use or overdose, repeated blood transfusions, liver disease such as hepatitis or cirrhosis, and certain types of anemia including hemolytic anemia, sideroblastic anemia, and thalassemia. A single elevated result does not confirm any of these conditions — your doctor will interpret it alongside your symptoms, medical history, and other test results. Discuss your high serum iron result with your healthcare provider before drawing any conclusions.

What causes low serum iron?

When serum iron falls below the normal range, it means less iron than expected is circulating in your blood. Common reasons include not getting enough iron through diet, blood loss (such as from heavy periods or gastrointestinal bleeding), iron deficiency anemia, chronic inflammatory conditions that cause the body to sequester iron, malabsorption disorders like celiac disease or Crohn's disease, pregnancy (which raises iron demand), chronic kidney disease, and hypothyroidism (an underactive thyroid gland). Because many of these causes require different approaches, a low result alone does not point to a single explanation. Your healthcare provider can order additional tests and review your history to identify what is driving your low level.

What symptoms are linked to abnormal serum iron levels?

Symptoms can go in two directions depending on whether iron is too low or too high. Low iron is often associated with fatigue and tiredness, pale skin, shortness of breath, brittle nails, hair loss, cold intolerance, difficulty concentrating (sometimes called brain fog), and restless leg syndrome. High iron, especially in chronic overload, can cause joint pain, fatigue and weakness, abdominal pain, skin bronzing or darkening, and liver enlargement — though mildly elevated levels are often symptom-free. Symptoms overlap with many other conditions, so experiencing any of these does not confirm an iron problem. Share any symptoms you are having with your healthcare provider so they can evaluate them alongside your test result.

Should I get more iron tests if my serum iron is abnormal?

Serum iron is rarely interpreted on its own. Because it only reflects circulating iron at one point in time, doctors commonly pair it with ferritin (which shows stored iron), transferrin saturation, and total iron-binding capacity (TIBC) to build a fuller picture of your iron status. The pattern across these tests helps distinguish between iron deficiency, chronic disease, and iron overload more reliably than any single value. Your result, your symptoms, your diet, and your medical history all factor into what comes next. Your healthcare provider is best placed to decide which follow-up tests, if any, make sense for your individual situation.
