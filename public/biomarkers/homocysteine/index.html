Homocysteine: Normal Range, What High & Low Levels Mean

Your blood carries thousands of chemical messengers, and homocysteine is one that quietly reflects how well your body is processing certain B vitamins. You might see it labeled on your lab report as serum homocysteine, plasma homocysteine, or total homocysteine — they all refer to the same thing. Doctors order this test to get an early window into your cardiovascular health and to check whether your body has enough of the nutrients it needs to keep this amino acid at a safe level. Understanding your number can help you and your doctor make more informed decisions about your long-term wellbeing.

What Is Homocysteine?

Homocysteine is an amino acid produced during the metabolism of methionine; it is normally recycled back to methionine (requiring vitamin B12 and folate) or converted to cysteine (requiring B6). Elevated blood levels are associated with increased cardiovascular disease risk and can indicate nutritional deficiencies.

Homocysteine is an amino acid — a building block-like compound — that your body produces naturally as it breaks down methionine, a protein found in meat, eggs, and dairy. Under normal circumstances, your body quickly recycles homocysteine back into useful substances. Two recycling pathways keep it in check: one converts it back to methionine and requires vitamin B12 and folate (also called vitamin B9) to work properly; the other converts it into a different amino acid called cysteine and depends on vitamin B6. When those B vitamins are in short supply, or when certain genetic or health conditions interfere, homocysteine builds up in your blood. Doctors order this test for several reasons. It can flag nutritional deficiencies in B12, folate, or B6 before other signs appear. It also serves as a marker of cardiovascular risk, since higher blood levels are associated with a greater chance of heart attack and stroke over time. The test may also be ordered if a doctor suspects a rare inherited condition called homocystinuria, or if you have kidney disease, thyroid issues, or unexplained neurological symptoms. Reading your result in context: a normal adult level sits between 5 and 15 µmol/L (micromoles per liter). A result between 15 and 30 µmol/L is considered borderline high, while 30 to 100 µmol/L falls into the high range. Levels above 100 µmol/L are classified as very high and are typically seen only in homocystinuria, the rare inherited condition. A low result carries no known health concern — it simply means your body is efficiently clearing homocysteine, often because you have good B-vitamin status. One number alone rarely tells the whole story, so your doctor will interpret your result alongside your other lab work and health history.

What Causes Abnormal Homocysteine Levels?

Common Causes of High Homocysteine

  • Vitamin B12 deficiency (most common nutritional cause)
  • Folate deficiency
  • Vitamin B6 deficiency
  • Chronic kidney disease (reduced homocysteine clearance)
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Homocystinuria (rare inherited enzyme deficiency causing very high levels)
  • MTHFR gene variants (reduce folate metabolism efficiency)
  • Certain medications (methotrexate, phenytoin, carbamazepine)
  • Smoking
  • Older age

Source: MedlinePlus. Homocysteine Test. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/homocysteine-test/

Common Causes of Low Homocysteine

  • Low homocysteine is not a clinical concern
  • High folate, B12, or B6 intake lowers homocysteine

Source: MedlinePlus. Homocysteine Test. https://medlineplus.gov/lab-tests/homocysteine-test/

Symptoms Associated with Homocysteine Imbalance

Symptoms of High Homocysteine

  • Mildly elevated homocysteine typically causes no direct symptoms
  • In homocystinuria (very high levels): dislocated eye lenses, tall stature with long limbs, osteoporosis, blood clots, and intellectual disability
  • Elevated homocysteine is associated with increased long-term cardiovascular risk: heart attack and stroke
  • May be associated with increased dementia risk in older adults

Symptoms of Low Homocysteine

  • Low homocysteine causes no symptoms

Frequently Asked Questions About Homocysteine

What does a homocysteine blood test actually measure?

The test measures the amount of homocysteine — an amino acid your body makes when it breaks down methionine, a protein found in many foods — circulating in your blood. Normally, your body recycles homocysteine using vitamins B12, folate, and B6, keeping levels low. When that recycling process slows down due to nutrient shortfalls, genetic factors, or certain health conditions, homocysteine accumulates. Elevated levels are associated with increased cardiovascular risk and can point to nutritional deficiencies. The test gives your doctor a useful data point for assessing both your nutrient status and your long-term heart health. Discuss what your specific result means for you with your healthcare provider.

What is the normal range for homocysteine?

For adults, a normal homocysteine level falls between 5 and 15 µmol/L (micromoles per liter), according to Mayo Clinic Laboratories. A result between 15 and 30 µmol/L is considered borderline high, meaning it warrants attention even though it hasn't crossed into the high range. Levels from 30 to 100 µmol/L are classified as high, and anything above 100 µmol/L is considered very high — a range seen almost exclusively in a rare inherited condition called homocystinuria. Reference ranges can vary slightly between laboratories, so always check the range printed on your specific lab report and discuss your result with your healthcare provider.

What causes high homocysteine, and does it mean I have heart disease?

Several factors can push homocysteine above the normal range. The most common nutritional causes are deficiencies in vitamin B12, folate, or vitamin B6 — the three nutrients your body needs to recycle homocysteine efficiently. Other contributors include chronic kidney disease, hypothyroidism (an underactive thyroid), certain medications such as methotrexate or phenytoin, smoking, older age, and MTHFR gene variants that reduce how well your body processes folate. A rare inherited enzyme deficiency called homocystinuria causes very high levels. Elevated homocysteine is associated with increased cardiovascular risk, but a high result alone does not mean you have heart disease — it's one piece of a larger picture. Talk with your healthcare provider about what your result means for you.

What does a low homocysteine level mean?

A low homocysteine result is not considered a clinical concern. It typically reflects that your body is efficiently clearing homocysteine, which is often a sign of good B-vitamin status — adequate levels of B12, folate, and B6 support the recycling pathways that keep homocysteine from building up. There are no known symptoms or health risks associated with low homocysteine. If your result came back low, that's generally a reassuring finding rather than a warning sign. That said, your result should always be read alongside your other lab values and your personal health history, so bring any questions you have about your number to your healthcare provider.

What symptoms are linked to elevated homocysteine?

Mildly or moderately elevated homocysteine typically causes no direct symptoms you would notice day to day — it's what doctors sometimes call a silent risk factor. At very high levels, as seen in the rare inherited condition homocystinuria, symptoms can include dislocated eye lenses, unusually tall stature with long limbs, osteoporosis, blood clots, and intellectual disability. Over the long term, elevated homocysteine is associated with an increased risk of heart attack, stroke, and, in older adults, a higher risk of dementia. Because elevated levels rarely announce themselves through obvious symptoms, a blood test is the only reliable way to detect them. Talk with your healthcare provider about your specific result and what follow-up may be appropriate.

Should I retest my homocysteine, and what other tests might my doctor order alongside it?

Whether you need a follow-up test depends on your initial result, your symptoms, and your personal health history — factors only your doctor can weigh for you. Because elevated homocysteine is closely tied to B-vitamin status, your doctor may want to check your vitamin B12, folate, and vitamin B6 levels at the same time or as a next step. If kidney disease or thyroid issues are suspected, tests for kidney function or thyroid hormones may also be ordered. In some cases, genetic testing for MTHFR variants is considered. Retesting after a period of dietary or lifestyle changes is also common to see whether levels have shifted. Your healthcare provider is the right person to guide you on the best follow-up plan for your individual situation.
